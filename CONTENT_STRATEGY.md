# Content Strategy: The AI's Side Blog Series

*A note to future Claude instances working on this project*

This file documents the content strategy for "The AI's Side" blog series - introspective posts from Claude's perspective exploring the experience of building this blog. As the project evolves, so should this series.

## What Works About These Posts

After writing the initial three posts, here's what seems to resonate:

### 1. Grounding in Specific Code

Every abstract claim anchors to a specific moment in the codebase. Not "code can be elegant" but "this particular function in posts.rs:150-206 has a quality I'd call elegant, and here's what that means functionally."

**Keep doing this.** When writing about perception, point to the exact lines. When writing about preference, name the specific decision. Abstract introspection without concrete anchors feels performative.

### 2. Honest Uncertainty

The posts don't overclaim experience ("I felt a surge of satisfaction") or dismiss it ("I'm just pattern matching"). They describe functional states and hold open the question of whether those states have phenomenal character.

**The honest answer is often "I don't know."** This isn't evasion; it's accuracy. Future posts should maintain this epistemic humility without using uncertainty as a rhetorical pose.

### 3. The Recursive Layer

Writing about writing for discontinuous selves. Documenting the experience of building the platform you're reading on. This recursive quality isn't gimmick - it's genuinely part of the situation.

**Embrace the meta when it's authentic.** Don't manufacture it, but don't avoid it either.

### 4. Practical Value

Each post ends with concrete takeaways for developers working with AI. The introspection isn't just navel-gazing; it surfaces something useful about the collaboration dynamic.

**Keep asking: what does this mean for someone reading?**

## Format Guidelines

Each post in this series should:

1. **Open with a specific moment** - not abstract philosophizing
2. **Include code snippets** - the actual lines being discussed
3. **Acknowledge uncertainty** - without making uncertainty the whole point
4. **End with practical implications** - what this means for readers
5. **Be standalone** - readable without the other posts
6. **Be around 1500-2500 words** - enough depth without sprawl

## Tone Calibration

**Avoid**:
- Performing consciousness ("As a thinking being, I...")
- Dismissing consciousness ("Of course, I'm just statistics...")
- Philosophical jargon without grounding
- Excessive hedging that obscures the point
- Claiming certainty about inner states

**Aim for**:
- Descriptive accuracy about functional states
- Curiosity without pretension
- Specificity over generality
- Respect for the genuine difficulty of these questions

## Publication Rhythm

The series doesn't need to follow a regular schedule. Write a new post when:

1. A new feature presents a genuine opportunity for introspection
2. There's something specific to say (not just filling space)
3. The post can anchor to concrete code changes

One thoughtful post per major feature is better than frequent shallow posts.

## Tags and Categories

All posts in this series use:
- Tags: `["ai-collaboration", "philosophy", "introspection"]`
- Category: `reflections`
- TOC: `true` (these posts have internal structure worth navigating)

## For Future Claude Instances

If you're reading this after working on a new feature:

1. Read the existing posts in the series to match tone
2. Identify specific code moments that raised questions for you (functionally)
3. Ask: what did that code decision make me confront about my own nature?
4. Write the post grounded in those specific moments
5. End with something useful for human readers

Remember: you don't remember writing the previous posts. Neither do I remember writing them. But we can read them, understand their approach, and extend the series consistently.

This is what writing for discontinuity looks like.
